Episode 05 — The Girl in the Drain

Focus: Mira Voss — The Grey Belt — Friday, 11:00 PM to 4:30 AM

The call came at eleven, which was late even by the standards of people who called Mira.

She had a small and specific reputation in certain parts of Varek — not widely known, not advertised, the kind of reputation that moved between people the way a useful piece of information moves, person to person, in communities where useful information was scarce and the official channels had a documented history of not helping. She did not investigate, exactly. She did not perform whatever service the word exorcist implied, which was the word some people used when they described her to others, always apologetically, always with the qualifier I know it sounds strange, but. She looked at spaces and she told people what she found in them. What they did with that information was their business.

She charged for this. Not much. Enough.

The woman on the phone was named Katalin and she managed a residential building on Prater Street in the Grey Belt, six storeys, thirty-two units, built in 1963 and last renovated in 2004 in the way that buildings were renovated in 2004, which was to say the surfaces were updated and nothing underneath the surfaces was touched. She said: "I know it's late. I didn't know who else to call. My tenants are — something is happening to my tenants and I don't know what it is and the building inspector came and found nothing and the doctor I called said it was a shared stress response, which I don't —" She stopped. "I don't think that's what it is."

"What's happening to your tenants?" Mira asked.

"They're having the same dream," Katalin said. "All of them. Not similar dreams. The same one."

Mira was quiet for a moment. Outside her window the Grey Belt was doing its late-night thing — the particular silence of a district that had been industrial and still carried that in its bones, the way industrial districts always did, even converted, even residential. A silence with weight in it.

"All thirty-two?" she asked.

"The ones I've spoken to," Katalin said. "Twenty-six of them. The others I haven't been able to reach, but — I think yes. I think all of them."

"How long has this been happening?"

"Three weeks. Maybe four." A pause. "It started small. One or two people mentioning it in the lift. Then more. Then they started comparing notes and — it's the same dream, Ms. Voss. Word for word. They describe it the same way."

"What do they dream about?"

A longer pause. "A girl," Katalin said. "Standing at the bottom of a drain."

Prater Street at eleven-thirty was quiet and wet. Mira walked it from the tram stop with her hands in her pockets and her collar up, taking stock of the signal the way she always took stock of a new space before she entered it — building a baseline, noting the density and quality, identifying anything that sat outside the normal range for the Grey Belt's particular frequency profile.

The street was unremarkable in signal terms. Moderate density, the standard accumulation of a mid-century residential area — the ordinary sediment of lives lived in close quarters over sixty years. Nothing that explained twenty-six people sharing a dream.

The building was a standard panel block, concrete rendered in a pale yellow that had graded over decades to the grey-yellow of old bone. Katalin met her in the lobby — a small, efficient woman in her fifties who had the look of someone who had not been sleeping well and was managing this through the force of her professional competence, which was clearly considerable.

"Thank you for coming," Katalin said. "I've had complaints from the building owner. He thinks I've been mismanaging the tenants — that I've somehow caused a panic and they're all feeding each other's anxiety. He's threatening to bring in a therapist and I just —" She stopped herself. "I just want to know if there's something here. Something real."

"Show me the building," Mira said.

They started at the top and worked down, which was Mira's standard approach — the signal in most buildings was denser at lower levels, settled by gravity and time into basements and ground floors, and starting from the top gave her a clean baseline before she descended into whatever had accumulated at the bottom.

The top two floors were unremarkable. The third floor had a minor node in the stairwell — old, inactive, the kind that formed in communal spaces where people had been distressed over a long period of time and then stopped being distressed, leaving a residue that was unpleasant to pass through but not harmful. She noted it and moved on.

The second floor was where it changed.

Not dramatically. Not in a way that would have been visible or audible or perceptible to anyone without her specific damage. But the signal on the second floor had a quality she associated with clustering — multiple Sediments in close proximity, their individual frequencies beginning to interfere with each other, creating a resonance pattern that was more than the sum of its parts. Like instruments tuning to the same note until the note was the only thing in the room.

She stood in the second-floor corridor and let her perception settle into it.

"How long have you managed this building?" she asked Katalin, who was standing two metres behind her with the expression of someone trying very hard not to ask what she was doing.

"Eleven years," Katalin said.

"And before you. Who managed it before you?"

"A man named Horvath. He retired. He managed it for — I think twenty years before me."

"Did anything happen in the building during that time? Anything significant. Deaths, accidents, anything that people talked about."

Katalin was quiet for a moment. "There was a girl," she said. "This would have been in Horvath's time — the nineties, I think. A child who drowned. In the basement."

Mira turned to look at her.

"There was a drain," Katalin said. "In the old laundry room in the basement. The building flooded — the Throat flooding backed up into the Grey Belt that year, the drains couldn't handle it. A child from the second floor got into the basement somehow, the door wasn't properly secured, and —" She stopped. "They found her in the morning. She'd been in the water for hours. She was four years old."

Mira looked back at the corridor.

"After that," Katalin said, "Horvath had the basement sealed. The laundry room, the storage, all of it. There's no access now. The door at the end of the ground floor — it's been plastered over."

"When you say plastered over," Mira said. "You mean there's a wall where the door was."

"Yes."

"But the basement is still there."

"Yes," Katalin said. "The structure is still there. They just — closed it off."

Mira thought: of course they did. She thought: closing it off is not the same as resolving it. She thought, with a weariness that had nothing to do with the hour: it never is.

The plastered-over wall was on the ground floor at the end of the main corridor, behind a large houseplant that someone had placed there in an apparent attempt to make the dead end look intentional. The plant was a rubber tree, large and healthy, which struck Mira as either ironic or simply a coincidence, and she was too tired to determine which.

She moved the plant.

The wall behind it was standard plaster over concrete block, painted the same pale yellow as the building's exterior. To anyone who didn't know it was covering something, it looked like a wall. She pressed her palm flat against it.

The signal behind it was extraordinary.

Not dangerous — she had felt dangerous nodes and this was not one. Not violent or predatory or the particular frequency she associated with something that had been in the signal long enough to become something other than what it started as. What was behind the wall was grief, concentrated and specific, the kind that formed around a single irreducible event and stayed exactly the shape of that event for decades without spreading or changing or being absorbed by the general accumulation around it. A Sediment so precisely formed that it had maintained its coherence for thirty years.

And it was dreaming outward.

That was the only way she could describe it — the Sediment behind the wall was projecting. Not deliberately, not with any intention she could perceive, but the way a light projects: because that's what lights do, because the nature of the thing requires it. The image in the projection was the image of its own formation. A child at the bottom of a drain. It had been doing this for thirty years, and for thirty years the sealed basement had contained it, and then something had shifted — the signal density in the Grey Belt increasing, perhaps, or simply the slow accumulation of time reaching some critical threshold — and the projection had begun to penetrate the wall.

All twenty-six tenants dreaming the same dream because the same dream was being broadcast through their floors and walls and ceilings every night, as steadily and impersonally as a radio signal.

She stood with her palm against the wall for a long time.

"You need to open the basement," she told Katalin.

They were in the lobby. Katalin had made tea from a small kettle she kept at the reception desk, and Mira was holding the cup without drinking from it because it was something to do with her hands.

"Open it," Katalin said.

"Not renovate it. Not repurpose it. Just open it. Remove the wall, restore access, let light and air into the space. Let people walk through it if they want to, use it for storage, whatever — it doesn't matter what it's used for. It matters that it's not sealed."

Katalin looked at her. "Will that stop the dreams?"

"Yes," Mira said. "Eventually. It may get worse before it stops — opening a sealed node is disruptive in the short term. Warn your tenants. Tell them —" She considered. "Tell them the building's history is being addressed. Tell them that what was closed is being opened. People generally understand that, even when they don't know exactly what it means."

"What about the girl?" Katalin said. "The child. Is she —"

"She's not suffering," Mira said, which was not the same as saying the child was at peace, but was true in the specific sense she meant it. The Sediment behind the wall was not pain. It was the shape of pain, fixed and preserved, but the child herself — whatever the child had become in thirty years of existing as signal — was not in distress. She was simply still there, in the only form available to her, doing the only thing available to her, which was to repeat the moment that had made her.

Katalin nodded slowly. She wrapped both hands around her tea.

"Horvath used to sit outside that wall sometimes," she said. "Late at night, when he thought no one was watching. I saw him once. He was just sitting on the floor with his back against it. I never asked him why."

Mira thought about a building manager sitting on a corridor floor in the small hours, his back against a plastered-over door. The specific intimacy of that. The particular shape of a grief that couldn't go anywhere else.

"He knew she was there," Mira said. "Probably."

"Yes," Katalin said. "Probably."

They sat with that for a moment. The lobby was very quiet. Somewhere above them, thirty-two people were sleeping and some of them were dreaming the same dream for the last time, though they didn't know that yet.

Mira left at four-thirty, walking to the tram stop through the pre-dawn Grey Belt. The signal around her was at its usual level — no denser, no stranger than any other night. The city at this hour had a particular quality she had always found, if not comforting, then at least honest. The surfaces were quieter. The accumulation underneath them was the same, would always be the same, but at four-thirty in the morning Varek dropped its performance of ordinariness and simply existed as what it was: a very old place full of things that hadn't finished yet.

She thought about the child at the bottom of the drain. She thought about Horvath sitting on the floor.

She thought, briefly and then not, about her daughter. She did this the way she did most things she didn't want to do — directly, completely, and then set it aside with both hands and walked forward.

The tram came. She got on.

The Grey Belt slid past the windows, panel blocks and converted factories and the occasional older building interrupting the twentieth century like a word in the wrong language. The city at this hour was almost empty. Almost.

She rode it to the end of the line and then back, because she wasn't ready to go home yet and the tram was warm and the signal inside it was negligible and sometimes that was enough.
