Five years before the asteroid that erased Elszia, an engineer named Lorenz Phis saw the pattern in the sky and could not make anyone listen. Five years of orbital data. Twenty-three objects too coordinated to be physics. By the time the impact came, his warning had cost him his name, his platform, and the option of being heard at all. That night, on dark water seventeen kilometers off the Marun coast, he did the one thing that would let him survive the next forty-nine years: he closed the part of his brain that loved his family.

He left a five-year-old son a hairpin and the only words that mattered.

Twelve years on.

Pell Phis is seventeen. A junior researcher chasing bounty work in a Marun institute that lost half its senior faculty to the evacuation chaos. His mother is dying slowly on a synthetic-neuron transplant the world handed out after the Impact. The hairpin his father pressed into his palm runs warm against his temple — a unit no Institute technician will admit to recognizing. He has been counting things since he was twelve. Failed substrate trays. Days since his missing friend's interface stopped answering. Steel still owed.
